# I Tried AI Faceless YouTube for 365 Days

**Inspired by:** "I Tried Youtube Shorts for 365 Days!" by Isaac (`https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X1WJlNUJOgg`, 4:58)
**Target:** ~5:00, 16:9, voiceover-led with fast cuts using the Isaac asset pack
**Word count target:** ~1000 words (Isaac's is 1024)

Beats mirror the source: hook → 4 numbered steps → formula breaks → crash → lesson = determination. Swap niche specifics freely.

## [0:00 – 0:28] HOOK

I've been running a faceless YouTube channel for over two years getting millions of views — until I realized I've been doing it completely wrong. There's a way to hit the same numbers in just a few months. Let me explain.

The channel I'm talking about is called **BotBrief**, where I post short and long-form videos about AI tools and how to actually use them. In this video, I'll focus on the short-form side, how you can get the same views as fast as possible, and the exact method I use to make them go viral.

> **B-roll:** Isaac character pose 1, glassy gradient background, channel logo flash, view-count counter ticking up, "DOWNLOAD IT" scribble overlay sparingly on transitions.

---

## [0:29 – 0:46] STEP 1 — TREAT YOUR CHANNEL LIKE A BRAND

Treat your channel as a brand. Decide what fonts and colors you'll use, and keep them consistent across every single video. To get started you need a logo and a banner — you can make them in any photo editor, or if you're not a designer, generate one with Midjourney like I did.

> **B-roll:** Isaac pose 2 holding "Step 1" card, Gradient1.png as background, Rectangle1.png as title plate, camera-shutter SFX on logo reveal.

---

## [0:47 – 1:09] STEP 2 — PICK A NICHE

If you're posting randomly without a clear topic, sorry — you can't succeed on this platform. Pick a niche. Make sure there are real people who want to watch content from that niche. You can ask ChatGPT for the most popular niches on YouTube, or open Wikipedia, search "list of hobbies", and pick one you're genuinely interested in. Then jump straight into step three.

> **B-roll:** Isaac pose 3 pointing, ChatGPT screen mock, Wikipedia screen mock, swoosh SFX between cuts.

---

## [1:09 – 2:04] STEP 3 — STUDY THE BIG CHANNELS AND SHIP FAST

You need to analyze the big channels in your niche and see what type of content is winning right now. I did exactly this when I started, and I saw "AI tool walkthroughs" doing really well. You've probably seen one — *"This AI tool replaces your video editor", "This AI tool builds your website in one prompt"*. So I picked a bunch of underrated tools and explained them as simply as I could.

One thing I strongly recommend: don't overthink it. Spend as little time per video as possible — especially at the start. You'll have plenty of time to improve later, but in the beginning, ship the first video as fast as you can. Get comfortable with that style, then pick **one thing** to improve next time. Five videos focused on better transitions. Then five on better subtitles. Then sound design, then color. It compounds way faster than trying to make everything perfect at once.

Back to the channel — after posting consistently for about three weeks, I got my first viral video.

> **B-roll:** Isaac pose 4 + 5 alternating, mouse-click SFX on screen mocks, typing SFX on subtitle examples, breaking-glass overlay on the "first viral video" beat.

---

## [2:06 – 2:55] STEP 4 — ANALYZE YOUR DATA AND BUILD A VIRAL RECIPE

After about ten shorts, go back and analyze the data. See which videos are winning and which aren't. This step is boring for some people but it's the most important one — don't skip it. The only metrics that matter for Shorts are view count, retention, and swipe rate. More importantly, rewatch your top videos and figure out what makes them different from the rest. Write it down as a "viral recipe" list.

You can either try to tick every box on the next short, or pick a few per video and stack them up. I made that list from my first viral short and noticed: the subtitles were shorter, the scene was changing faster, and I made a joke at the end that wasn't even funny. I applied the list to my next batch and got two more viral shorts that same month. By December 2024, I finally got monetized.

> **B-roll:** YouTube analytics screen mock with retention curve, gradient9 background, riser SFX building into the "monetized" beat.

---

## [3:00 – 4:00] THE TWIST — THE FORMULA BREAKS

What worked in the past won't always work today. After running the same formula for another month, I noticed views were dropping on every new video. Even though the channel was monetized, ad revenue was under twenty dollars a month. Things weren't looking good.

If you hit that ceiling on your channel too — that's the signal that your golden formula is broken, or needs a revision. Repeat steps three and four. Add new entries to your viral recipe list.

So I did it again. This time I noticed: the best-performing videos in my niche had a sound effect right in the first second — *like this* — some kind of bold graphic that grabbed attention, and most of them didn't even use a voiceover. They were just clean edits with on-screen text.

I was suspicious because that style was way easier to make. But I made a bunch of them anyway, and they did really well. The channel started gaining real traction. Ad revenue went from twenty to over a hundred and fifty dollars in that month. Everything was great. I was sure it was about to turn into thousands a month within weeks.

Until one day I got an email from YouTube.

> **B-roll:** Distortion SFX at "the formula breaks", neon overlay during the second-attempt montage, click-tap SFX on UI shots, riser building into the email cliffhanger.

---

## [4:00 – 4:35] THE CRASH

It said: *some of your videos have been taken down due to a copyright notice.* Three of my best-performing edits got pulled. My channel got a copyright strike. And a few hours later, the channel got demonetized for **reused content** — turns out the edits weren't transformed enough to qualify as fair use.

It was the worst thing that could've happened — right when the channel was starting to take off.

> **B-roll:** Breaking-glass overlay on "copyright notice", distortion SFX on the strike beat, Isaac pose 7 with hands-up reaction, demonetized badge graphic.

---

## [4:35 – 4:58] THE LESSON — DETERMINATION

But that didn't stop me from posting. I went back to voiceovers, ran steps three and four again to make the formula even stronger, and I'm reapplying to the YouTube Partner Program in a few weeks.

If there's one principle to take from this video, it's **determination**. No matter how brutal it gets — learn from each one, find a fix, and don't lose the motivation.

> **B-roll:** Isaac character on plain gradient, slow zoom, ambient riser tail, fade to channel logo with swoosh.
